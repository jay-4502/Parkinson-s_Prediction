Discovering Parkinson's Disease

Parkinson's motor symptoms, including tremors and slow movements, influence tasks like drawing and writing, evident in irregular patterns. Changes in voice tone and fluidity also assist in diagnosis. Analyzing these cues aids early detection, guiding personalized treatment plans for improved patient well-being.

Tremors

Involuntary shaking or trembling of the hands, arms, legs, jaw, or face, especially when at rest. Tremors are often one of the first symptoms noticed in Parkinson's disease.

Bradykinesia

Slowness of movement or difficulty initiating movement. People with Parkinson's disease may find it challenging to perform everyday tasks such as walking, getting out of a chair, or writing. Movements may also become smaller and less fluid.

Rigidity

Stiffness or inflexibility of the limbs or trunk, making it difficult to move freely. Rigidity can lead to muscle aches and pains, and it may affect both sides of the body symmetrically. This stiffness can contribute to a stooped posture and reduced range of motion.

Problems with Showing Emotion

Difficulty in expressing emotions through facial expressions, such as smiling or frowning. Parkinson's disease may affect the muscles of the face, resulting in a reduced range of facial expressions. This can make it challenging to convey emotions effectively, which may affect social interactions.
